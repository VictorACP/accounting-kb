Welcome to the Portfolio Accounting Knowledge Base. This guide is written for team members at portfolio companies who perform accounting tasks in QBO but may not have a formal accounting background. Each section includes the policy (the why and the rules) followed by step-by-step QBO instructions (the how). When in doubt, follow the policy exactly — consistency across companies is the goal.

Golden Rules

These principles apply across every portfolio company, regardless of COA differences.

📋 Always Match the Approval

No bill or payment should be entered in QBO without a corresponding approved document (PO, invoice, or email sign-off). Attach the source document in QBO at time of entry.

🗓 Use the Correct Date

Always use the vendor invoice date for bills and the actual receipt date for payments — not today's date unless they're the same. Period-end accuracy depends on this.

🏷 Code to the Right Account

Each company has its own chart of accounts. If you're unsure which account to use, refer to the COA mapping document or ask the controller before posting.

🔄 No Manual Bank Adjustments

Never adjust a bank balance manually. All differences must be explained through reconciling items, and unresolved items must be escalated within 3 business days of month-end.

Accounts Payable Policy

Purpose

This policy ensures all vendor obligations are recorded accurately, in the correct period, and with proper documentation — so that cash flow projections, aging reports, and financial statements are reliable across all portfolio companies.

Coding Rules

Account Classification

  • Goods or services fully consumed in the current period: expense account in the period received.
  • Goods received but not yet received/verified: hold in Accrued Liabilities until confirmed, then reclassify.
  • Prepaid expenses (e.g. insurance, subscriptions >1 month): record to Prepaid Expenses asset account and amortize monthly.
  • Capital items over $2,500: do not expense — notify controller for capitalization treatment.
Payment Terms & Timing

Default Terms

  • Pay vendors within stated terms (Net 30 is default unless contract differs).
  • Early payment discounts (e.g. 2/10 Net 30) must be approved by controller before taking — record the discount to Purchase Discounts or as a reduction of the expense.
  • Do not pay a bill that does not match an approved PO or invoice. Escalate discrepancies immediately.
⚠ Duplicate Payment Risk: Before entering a vendor bill, search QBO for the vendor name and confirm the invoice number has not already been entered. Duplicate invoice numbers from the same vendor are a red flag — do not override the warning.
Entering Vendor Bills in QBO How-To

Follow these steps every time you receive a vendor invoice that needs to be paid. A "bill" in QBO creates an amount owed — it does not move cash until you record a payment.

Before you start: Make sure you have the physical or digital vendor invoice in hand, approval has been obtained per the AP Policy, and you know which expense account to use. If unsure on the account, check with the controller.

  1. Navigate to Bills. From the QBO left menu, click + New → Bill (under Vendors column).

  2. Select the Vendor. In the Vendor dropdown, search for and select the vendor. If the vendor is not listed, stop — new vendors require controller approval before setup.

  3. Set the Bill Date. Enter the date printed on the vendor's invoice — not today's date unless they match. This determines which accounting period the expense falls in.

  4. Enter the Due Date. QBO may auto-fill this based on saved terms. Verify it matches the invoice. Correct it if needed.

  5. Enter the Invoice/Reference Number. Type the vendor's invoice number exactly as it appears in the Bill no. field. This is critical for preventing duplicates and for vendor queries.

  6. Add Line Items. In the table at the bottom, for each line: select the correct Account, enter a Description (be specific — e.g. "Office supplies – March 2025" not just "supplies"), and enter the Amount.

  7. Apply Class/Location (if your company uses them). If your QBO is set up with Class or Location tracking, select the appropriate values on each line. Do not leave these blank if they are required fields.

  8. Attach the Invoice. Click the Attachments paperclip icon and upload a scan or PDF of the vendor invoice. This is required for all bills.

  9. Add a Memo. In the Memo field at the bottom, note who approved the bill and the date of approval (e.g. "Approved by J. Smith 2025-03-10").

  10. Save. Click Save and close. The bill will now appear in the AP aging and is ready to be paid when due.

⚠ Common Errors: Using today's date instead of the invoice date; leaving the bill number blank; coding the full amount of a multi-period contract to a single period expense. When in doubt, ask before saving.
Recording Bill Payments in QBO How-To

Use this process when a payment has been made (check cleared, ACH sent, wire confirmed). Do not record a payment until the money has actually left the bank account.

  1. Navigate to Pay Bills. Click + New → Pay Bills (under Vendors).

  2. Select the Payment Account. In the Payment account dropdown, select the correct bank account from which payment was made. If payment was via credit card, use the credit card account instead.

  3. Set the Payment Date. Enter the date the payment actually left your account (ACH send date, check date, wire date). Not the invoice due date.

  4. Find the Bill(s) to Pay. The list will show all open bills. Check the box next to the bill(s) this payment covers.

  5. Verify the Amount. Confirm the Payment column matches what was actually paid. If you're making a partial payment, edit the amount in that field.

  6. Enter Check/Reference Number. In the Ref no. field, enter the check number, ACH confirmation number, or wire reference. This is essential for bank reconciliation.

  7. Save. Click Save and close. The bill status will update to Paid and the payment will appear in the bank account register.

Partial Payments: If paying only part of a bill, enter the partial amount in the Payment column. The remaining balance will stay open in AP aging. Always note the reason in the Memo field (e.g. "Partial payment per agreement with vendor – balance due 4/15").
⚠ Never use "Write Check" to pay a bill that was already entered as a Bill. This creates a duplicate expense. Always use Pay Bills to clear open bill balances.
Vendor Credit Memos How-To

A vendor credit memo reduces the amount you owe a vendor — it may result from a returned item, billing error, or agreed discount. Never delete a bill to correct it; use a credit memo instead.

Creating a Vendor Credit

  1. Navigate to Vendor Credit. Click + New → Vendor Credit (under Vendors).

  2. Select the Vendor. Choose the same vendor as the original bill.

  3. Enter the Credit Date. Use the date on the vendor's credit memo document.

  4. Enter the Vendor's Credit Reference Number in the Ref no. field.

  5. Code to the Same Account. Use the same expense account as the original bill. This properly reverses the cost. If the credit relates to a returned inventory item, use the Inventory account.

  6. Enter the Amount and a clear Description (e.g. "Return of defective units per RMA#1234").

  7. Attach the vendor's credit memo document.

  8. Save. Click Save and close. The credit will now appear as an open credit for this vendor.

Applying a Vendor Credit to a Bill

  1. Go to + New → Pay Bills.

  2. Select the vendor and find the bill you want to apply the credit to.

  3. QBO will display an open credit at the bottom of the screen. Check the credit to apply it. The payment amount will reduce automatically.

  4. Pay the remaining balance (if any) and save.

Credits vs. Refunds: If the vendor is sending you actual cash back (a check or bank deposit), do not use Vendor Credit — use Deposit and code to the original expense account. Ask the controller if you're unsure which applies.
AP Aging Reports in QBO How-To

The AP Aging report shows all unpaid vendor bills organized by how overdue they are. Review this at minimum weekly, and always before month-end close.

  1. From the left menu, click Reports.

  2. In the search bar, type Accounts Payable Aging and select Accounts Payable Aging Detail (the detail version shows individual bills; the summary shows totals by vendor).

  3. Set the Report date to today (or the last day of the period you're reviewing).

  4. Set Aging method to Report date (not "Current date") so the report is reproducible.

  5. Click Run report.

  6. Review each column: Current, 1–30, 31–60, 61–90, 91+. Any bill in the 61+ columns needs immediate attention.

  7. Export to Excel via Export → Export to Excel if you need to share with the controller.

Escalation Rules

  • Any bill over 60 days past due: notify the controller within 24 hours.
  • Any bill over $10,000 outstanding for more than 30 days: flag for review.
  • Bills with a zero or negative balance showing in aging: investigate — this may indicate a misapplied payment or unapplied credit. Do not ignore these.
Accounts Receivable Policy

Purpose

This policy ensures revenue is recognized in the correct period, customer balances are collected on time, and the AR aging is accurate for cash flow forecasting and financial reporting.

Invoice Issuance Rules

When to Invoice

  • Invoice upon delivery of goods or completion of services — not when payment is received.
  • For long-term contracts with milestones, invoice only when the milestone is met and documented.
  • Recurring invoices (subscriptions, retainers) must be sent on the first business day of the billing period.
  • Never backdate an invoice into a prior closed period without controller approval.

Collections

Follow-Up Schedule

  • Day 31 (1 day overdue): Send a courteous reminder email — QBO can automate this.
  • Day 45: Phone call + email from the local team.
  • Day 60: Escalate to controller. No additional goods or services to be extended to this customer until resolved.
  • Day 90+: Controller determines write-off or collections agency referral.
⚠ Write-offs require controller approval — never apply a credit or write off a balance without explicit sign-off, even for small amounts.
Processing Customer Invoices in QBO How-To

Creating an invoice in QBO records that a customer owes you money. It does not record cash — that happens when you receive payment.

  1. Navigate to Invoice. Click + New → Invoice (under Customers).

  2. Select the Customer. Search for the customer in the Customer dropdown. If they don't exist, stop and follow the new customer setup process — do not create on-the-fly without approval.

  3. Set the Invoice Date. Enter the date goods were delivered or services were completed. This is the revenue recognition date.

  4. Set the Due Date / Payment Terms. Verify the terms match what is in the customer's contract or the AR policy. Adjust if needed.

  5. Add a unique Invoice Number. QBO auto-numbers invoices — do not change the sequence unless instructed by the controller.

  6. Add Line Items. For each product or service: select the Product/Service, enter a Description, Quantity, and Rate. The income account is pre-mapped to the product/service item — do not override it unless you are certain.

  7. Apply Tax (if applicable). If sales tax applies, ensure the correct tax code is selected per your state/locality.

  8. Add a Customer Message if needed (e.g. payment instructions, PO reference number the customer requires).

  9. Save and Send. Click Save and send to email the invoice to the customer, or Save and close if sending manually.

Product/Service Items Matter: Revenue is coded based on the Product/Service item selected, not the line description. Always pick the correct item — if you're unsure, ask before saving.
Receiving Customer Payments in QBO How-To

Record a customer payment as soon as you confirm the funds have been received — ACH cleared, check deposited, or wire confirmed. Do not record anticipated payments.

  1. Navigate to Receive Payment. Click + New → Receive Payment (under Customers).

  2. Select the Customer. All open invoices for this customer will appear below.

  3. Set the Payment Date. Use the date the payment was actually received/cleared, not the invoice date.

  4. Select Payment Method. Choose: Check, ACH/EFT, Credit Card, Wire, or Cash. This is required for bank reconciliation accuracy.

  5. Enter Reference Number. Check number, wire reference, or ACH trace number. Leave blank for cash.

  6. Select the Deposit To Account. Choose the correct bank account. If checks are being batched for deposit, select Undeposited Funds and record the actual deposit separately when it clears.

  7. Check the Invoice(s) Being Paid. Tick the boxes next to the invoices this payment covers. QBO will auto-fill amounts.

  8. Handle Partial Payments. If the customer paid less than the full amount, edit the Payment column to the actual amount received. Leave the balance open — do not write it off without controller approval.

  9. Save. Click Save and close.

⚠ Undeposited Funds: If you use Undeposited Funds as the "deposit to" account, you must then record the actual bank deposit via + New → Bank Deposit. Leaving items in Undeposited Funds without clearing them is one of the most common causes of bank reconciliation errors.
Customer Credit Memos How-To

A credit memo reduces what a customer owes you. Issue one for returns, billing errors, or goodwill adjustments — with controller approval for any amount over $500.

Creating a Credit Memo

  1. Click + New → Credit Memo (under Customers).

  2. Select the Customer.

  3. Set the Credit Memo Date — use the date the credit is being issued, not the original invoice date.

  4. Add line items using the same Product/Service as the original invoice. This reverses the revenue correctly.

  5. In the Message field, note the reason (e.g., "Return per RMA #456 — controller approved 3/10/25").

  6. Click Save and close.

Applying a Credit Memo

  1. Open the customer's next invoice or go to Receive Payment for this customer.

  2. QBO will show the available credit at the bottom. Check the credit to apply it against an open invoice.

  3. If the credit exceeds the invoice balance, the remainder stays as an open credit on the customer account until applied to a future invoice.

Refund vs. Credit: If you are sending money back to the customer (check or bank transfer), use + New → Refund Receipt instead. Ask the controller which applies for your situation.
AR Aging Reports in QBO How-To

The AR Aging report shows all unpaid customer invoices by how overdue they are. Run this weekly and send to the controller before month-end.

  1. From the left menu, click Reports.

  2. Search for Accounts Receivable Aging. Select Accounts Receivable Aging Detail.

  3. Set Report date to today or the last day of the month.

  4. Set Aging method to Report date.

  5. Click Run report.

  6. Review columns: Current, 1–30, 31–60, 61–90, 91+. Flag any balance in the 61+ columns for follow-up per the AR Policy.

  7. Export via Export → Export to Excel.

What to Look For

  • Negative balances in aging = overpayment or unapplied credit — investigate immediately.
  • Same customer with many small overdue balances = may indicate disputed invoices — escalate.
  • Very old invoices (91+) = need controller review for write-off consideration.
Intercompany Transactions Policy

Purpose

Intercompany (IC) transactions occur when one portfolio company buys from, sells to, loans money to, or incurs costs on behalf of another. These must be recorded identically on both sides so they eliminate cleanly when the controller consolidates financial statements. Errors here cause the most significant audit and reporting problems.

Core Rules

The Mirror Principle

  • Every IC transaction must be recorded in both companies in QBO before month-end close.
  • The amount, date, and description must be identical in both entities.
  • One company records an IC Receivable; the other records an IC Payable — using dedicated IC accounts (do not use general AR/AP).
  • All IC transactions require a written reference (email, shared memo, or IC transaction log) that both sides can point to.
Dedicated Account Codes
Because each portfolio company has its own COA, contact the controller to confirm the exact account numbers used for Intercompany Receivable, Intercompany Payable, and Intercompany Revenue/Expense at your specific entity. Do not use generic AR or AP accounts for IC transactions.
Transaction TypeSending Company BooksReceiving Company Books
Cash transfer / loanDebit: IC ReceivableCredit: IC Payable
Goods sold between entitiesDebit: IC Receivable / Credit: IC RevenueDebit: IC Expense / Credit: IC Payable
Shared cost allocation (e.g. rent, IT)Debit: IC Receivable / Credit: ExpenseDebit: Expense / Credit: IC Payable
Management feeDebit: IC Receivable / Credit: Management Fee IncomeDebit: Management Fee Expense / Credit: IC Payable
Monthly IC Reconciliation

Required Before Month-End Close

  • Both sides must confirm their IC balances match — the controller sends a reconciliation template on the 25th of each month.
  • Unreconciled IC differences must be explained and resolved before the books are closed. Do not let differences roll forward.
  • IC transactions must never be partially recorded (e.g., only one side books the entry).
Recording Intercompany Transactions in QBO How-To

There is no automated IC feature in QBO — you must make journal entries or use IC-specific bill/invoice workflows manually on both sides. Below is the most reliable method.

Always confirm the IC accounts with the controller before your first intercompany entry. Using the wrong account codes will cause consolidation errors that are difficult to unwind.
Method: Journal Entry (Most Common)

Used for cost allocations, management fees, and cash transfers. Must be entered in both entities.

Company A (Sender)

Click + New → Journal Entry

Line 1: Debit IC Receivable – [Company B]
Line 2: Credit relevant income or expense account

Memo: "IC allocation to [Company B] – [Month] – per [reference]"

Company B (Receiver)

Click + New → Journal Entry

Line 1: Debit relevant expense account
Line 2: Credit IC Payable – [Company A]

Memo: "IC allocation from [Company A] – [Month] – per [reference]"

Date and amount must match Company A exactly.

Method: IC Invoice/Bill Workflow

Used when goods or services flow between entities and you want a paper trail with an invoice number.

  1. In Company A (selling entity): create a standard Invoice to a customer named "[Company B] – Intercompany." Use the IC Revenue account, not regular revenue.

  2. In Company B (buying entity): create a Bill from vendor "[Company A] – Intercompany." Use the IC Expense account. Reference Company A's invoice number in the Bill no. field.

  3. When cash settles between entities, record the payment on both sides — Pay Bills in Company B and Receive Payment in Company A — on the same date.

⚠ Month-End Check: Pull the IC Receivable balance from Company A and the IC Payable balance from Company B. They must be equal. If they differ, do not close the books — investigate and correct the discrepancy first.
Inventory Policy

Purpose

Consistent inventory accounting ensures that cost of goods sold (COGS) is recorded in the same period as the related revenue, that balance sheet inventory values are accurate, and that physical counts align with the books.

Inventory Valuation Method

Method: FIFO (First In, First Out)

All portfolio companies use FIFO unless the controller has specifically approved an alternative in writing. QBO's inventory module uses average cost by default — confirm with the controller how your entity is set up. Do not change the inventory valuation method in QBO settings without controller approval.

Capitalization of Inventory Costs

What Goes Into Inventory Cost

  • Purchase price of goods (net of trade discounts)
  • Freight-in and import duties
  • Applicable handling costs
  • Not included: storage costs after receipt, freight-out (selling expense), or administrative overhead
Inventory Write-Downs & Obsolescence

Rules

  • Inventory may only be written down (not up) — do not increase inventory values above cost without controller sign-off.
  • Items that have not moved in 180 days must be flagged for review by the controller.
  • Write-downs require controller approval and must be documented with a reason (damaged, obsolete, excess).
  • Physical counts must be reconciled to QBO at least quarterly. Variances over $500 or 5% of total inventory value (whichever is lower) must be investigated before adjustment.
Purchase Orders & Receiving Inventory in QBO How-To

The PO workflow creates a three-way match: Purchase Order → Item Receipt → Vendor Bill. All three steps must be completed for inventory purchases.

Step 1 — Create a Purchase Order

  1. Click + New → Purchase Order.

  2. Select the Vendor and set the PO Date and expected Ship-by date.

  3. Add line items: select the inventory Product/Service item (not an account), enter Quantity and Rate. QBO will link the cost to inventory automatically via the item setup.

  4. Add a Memo with any special instructions or the internal approval reference.

  5. Click Save and close. Send the PO to the vendor.

Step 2 — Receive Items

  1. When goods physically arrive, open the original PO: Expenses → Purchase Orders → [find PO].

  2. Click Copy to → Item Receipt (or Receive Items depending on your QBO version/settings).

  3. Adjust quantities to reflect what was actually received (may differ from PO). Note any shortages or overages in the Memo field.

  4. Set the receipt date to today (the date goods arrived — this is when inventory hits your books).

  5. Save. Inventory balances will increase immediately.

Step 3 — Enter the Vendor Bill

  1. When the vendor invoice arrives, open the PO again and click Copy to → Bill.

  2. Verify amounts match the vendor invoice. Investigate any discrepancy before saving.

  3. Set the Bill Date to the vendor invoice date.

  4. Attach the vendor invoice. Save.

Three-Way Match: PO quantity = Received quantity = Billed quantity. Discrepancies must be resolved before paying the bill. Never pay a bill that doesn't match what was received.
Recording COGS in QBO How-To

In QBO with inventory tracking enabled, COGS is recorded automatically when you create a sales invoice or sales receipt using an inventory item. Understanding this flow prevents double-counting errors.

How COGS Flows Automatically

The Automatic Entry (When You Invoice a Customer)

When you sell an inventory item via an invoice or sales receipt, QBO makes two simultaneous entries:

  • Revenue side: Debit Accounts Receivable / Credit Sales Revenue
  • COGS side: Debit COGS / Credit Inventory Asset

You do not need to manually create a COGS journal entry. If someone has been doing this manually, they were likely double-counting — alert the controller.

When Manual COGS Entries Are Needed

Exceptions Only — Controller Approval Required

  • Goods given away as samples or promotional items (not invoiced)
  • Internal use of inventory (consumed in operations)
  • Period-end COGS adjustment if inventory count reveals a variance
Manual COGS Journal Entry (Exceptions Only)

  1. Click + New → Journal Entry.

  2. Line 1: Debit Cost of Goods Sold — enter the cost value of inventory removed.

  3. Line 2: Credit Inventory Asset — same amount.

  4. In the Memo field, state the reason clearly: "Samples given to XYZ Corp — 50 units @ $12.00 cost — approved by controller [date]."

  5. Attach any supporting documentation. Save.

⚠ Never credit revenue or debit COGS without also moving the inventory asset. An unbalanced COGS entry will cause inventory values on the balance sheet to be incorrect.
Inventory Adjustments in QBO How-To

Inventory adjustments are used when the physical count differs from QBO records (shrinkage, damage, count errors). All adjustments require controller approval.

⚠ Required Before Adjusting: You must have (1) a completed physical count sheet signed by the person who did the count, (2) comparison to QBO on-hand quantities, and (3) written controller approval specifying the approved variance to adjust.
Adjusting Inventory Quantity

  1. From the left menu, go to Inventory (or Products and Services → Inventory).

  2. Click Adjust Quantity (or + New → Inventory Qty Adjustment).

  3. Set the Adjustment Date to the date the physical count was performed.

  4. Select the Inventory Adjustment Account — this should be a dedicated account (e.g. "Inventory Shrinkage & Adjustments" or similar). Confirm the correct account with the controller. Do not use COGS directly.

  5. For each item being adjusted: find the item in the list, and in the New QTY on Hand column, enter the correct quantity from the physical count. QBO will calculate the difference.

  6. In the Memo field, note: the count date, who performed the count, and the controller approval reference.

  7. Click Save and close. The inventory asset and adjustment account will update automatically.

Adjusting for Damaged / Obsolete Inventory

  1. Follow the same steps above, reducing quantity to zero (or the salvageable amount) for damaged/obsolete items.

  2. Use the Inventory Write-Down or Inventory Obsolescence account (confirm name with controller) — not the standard adjustment account.

  3. Attach photos or written documentation of the damaged goods if available.

Audit Trail: QBO keeps a log of all inventory adjustments. Do not attempt to delete or edit a posted adjustment — if an error was made, create a correcting entry and note the reason in the memo. Notify the controller of any corrections.
